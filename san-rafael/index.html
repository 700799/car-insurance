1 What California requires

Every driver in California must carry proof of financial responsibility — almost always an auto liability policy. As of January 1, 2025, the state minimum limits rose for the first time since 1967.

  • $30,000 — Bodily injury / death — one person
  • $60,000 — Bodily injury / death — per accident
  • $15,000 — Property damage

Written as 30/60/15 (SB 1107, the “Protect California Drivers Act”). The old 15/30/5 minimums are no longer compliant.

The basics

  • Liability only is the legal minimum. It pays for injuries and damage you cause to others — not your own car.
  • California is an at-fault (tort) state. The driver who causes a crash is responsible. There is no “no-fault” / PIP requirement here; personal-injury protection is not part of a California policy.
  • Insurers report to the DMV electronically. Carry your insurance card; a lapse can trigger a registration suspension.
  • Driving uninsured is costly. Fines start around $100–$200 but climb past $450 with penalty assessments, and your vehicle can be impounded.

Minimum limits are a floor, not a goal. A single serious injury can blow past $30,000 in minutes — most Bay Area drivers should carry higher liability limits.

2 Real Bay Area situations

Four situations Bay Area teens and new drivers actually face, and the coverage that mattered.

At-fault rear-end, Oakland / I-880

New driver · liability

A new driver rear-ended an SUV on I-880. The other party’s repairs and a minor injury claim quickly approached the state minimum. With only 30/60/15, there was little room to spare.

Lesson: California’s minimums are low. Higher bodily-injury limits (e.g. 100/300) protect your savings if you’re at fault.

Uninsured driver, San Francisco

Hit by uninsured · UM

A driver with no insurance ran a light in San Francisco and fled. About one in six California drivers is uninsured, so there was no one to bill. Uninsured-motorist coverage paid the medical bills and repairs.

Lesson: California lets you waive uninsured-motorist coverage in writing — don’t. It’s inexpensive and especially valuable in dense Bay Area traffic.

Teen on the family policy

Teen · discounts

Instead of a standalone policy (often $7,000+ a year), a 17-year-old was added to a parent’s policy and kept a B average for the good-student discount, cutting the added cost substantially.

Lesson: Stay on a parent’s policy and stack the good-student discount. After three clean years, the 20% Good Driver Discount applies.

Paid-off commuter car

Older car · trade-off

A 12-year-old car worth about $3,000 still carried collision and comprehensive. The premiums plus the deductible were nearing the car’s value, so the owner dropped them and kept strong liability.

Lesson: When collision + comprehensive cost more than ~10% of the car’s value, dropping them often makes sense.

3 What sets your rate in the Bay Area

California is unusual. Under Proposition 103 (1988), the Department of Insurance must approve rates in advance, and insurers must weight three mandatory factors most heavily — in this order. Bay Area drivers tend to pay above the state average due to higher vehicle density and theft rates, but Prop 103’s protections apply statewide.

  • ~$2,700/yr Avg. full coverage (2026)
  • ~$700/yr Avg. minimum coverage
  • 40–50% Rate rise over ~2 years
  • ≥20% Mandated Good Driver Discount

The three mandatory factors (in order of weight)

  1. 1. Driving safety record. Accidents and violations carry the most weight. A clean record is the single biggest lever you control.
  2. 2. Annual miles driven. California explicitly rewards lower mileage. Fewer miles means lower risk and a lower rate.
  3. 3. Years of driving experience. More experience lowers your rate over time — a major reason new drivers pay the most.

What California prohibits

  • Credit score. Cannot be used for auto insurance in California.
  • Gender. Banned as a rating factor since 2019.
  • ZIP code as the primary factor. Location may be a secondary factor only — it can’t outweigh the three mandatory ones. Bay Area ZIPs often carry higher rates as a secondary factor.

Rates have climbed sharply (rising repair costs, claims severity, and wildfire-driven losses across the market). Bay Area drivers typically pay 10–30% above the state average depending on city and ZIP. Optional secondary factors insurers may use include vehicle type, marital status, and annual mileage bands — but never credit or gender.

6 Get the best rate (and what to avoid)

How to get the lowest legitimate rate in the Bay Area — built around the way Prop 103 actually prices policies. These are the moves that move the needle, plus the traps that quietly cost you.

Do this — the moves that lower your rate

  • Compare at least 3–5 real quotes California rates for identical coverage can differ by well over 100% between insurers. Comparison shopping is the single biggest lever — re-quote every 1–2 years and after any life change.
  • Always claim the Good Driver Discount Prop 103 guarantees ≥20% off for ~3 years’ clean driving (no more than one point). It’s the law — make sure every quote applies it.
  • Report your true (low) mileage Miles driven is the #2 mandatory factor in California. If you drive less than you used to — remote work, a short commute — update it. Verified-mileage and telematics programs can cut more.
  • Raise your deductible to ‘oops’ money Going from $250 to $1,000 can drop collision/comp 15–30%. Pick the highest deductible you could comfortably pay tomorrow and pocket the savings.
  • Right-size coverage to the car On a paid-off car worth under ~$4k, dropping collision/comprehensive often beats paying for them (the 10% rule). Keep strong liability + UM regardless.
  • Stack every discount you qualify for Bundle auto with renters/home, multi-car, good-student (B average), driver-training, paperless/auto-pay, and defensive-driving course credits. Ask explicitly — they aren’t always automatic.
  • Keep continuous coverage Even one lapse raises your rate and can suspend your registration. If you sell a car, keep a non-owner policy or transfer coverage rather than going bare.
  • Pay in full and go paperless Many California insurers offer a paid-in-full discount and small e-billing credits. If you pay monthly, compare the installment fees.

Avoid this — the quiet money-wasters

  • Auto-renewing for years ‘Loyalty’ often means price creep. Insurers bank on inertia — the renewal quote is rarely your best quote.
  • Buying only the state minimum by default 30/60/15 is cheap but thin; one serious injury blows past it and you pay the rest. Don’t confuse ‘cheapest premium’ with ‘best value’.
  • Waiving uninsured-motorist coverage California lets you reject UM/UIM in writing. Don’t — roughly 1 in 6 California drivers is uninsured, and UM is inexpensive.
  • Letting a small claim raise your rate Filing a claim that’s barely above your deductible can cost you more in surcharges than it pays. For minor damage, get an estimate first.
  • Guessing your mileage too high Overstating annual miles inflates your premium under California’s rules. Use your real odometer trend.
  • Ignoring the car you buy Insurance cost varies hugely by model (repair cost, theft rates, EV battery repair). Get a quote before you buy the car, not after.

What you can’t be charged for in California: your credit score and your gender — both are banned. Be skeptical of any ‘tip’ or quote that leans on those.

7 7 common myths

Misconceptions cost Bay Area drivers real money. Here are seven of the most common — and the truth, with the California angle.

Myth: Red cars cost more to insure.

Truth: Color is not a rating factor anywhere — and California can’t even use it. Insurers rate on make, model, trim, engine, repair cost, and theft rates, never paint color.

Myth: My credit score affects my California rate.

Truth: Not here. California is one of a handful of states (with MA, HI, MI) that bans credit-based insurance scores for auto. Your credit can’t legally raise your premium.

Myth: Men always pay more than women (or vice-versa).

Truth: California banned gender as an auto rating factor in 2019. Two otherwise-identical drivers can’t be priced differently on gender here.

Myth: The state minimum means I’m fully protected.

Truth: 30/60/15 is the legal floor, not real protection. A single ER visit or a totaled newer car can exceed it — and you personally owe the rest.

Myth: Older / low-value cars don’t need any coverage.

Truth: You still must carry liability to drive legally in California, even on a $500 beater. You can drop collision/comp on it — but never liability.

Myth: My insurer automatically gives me every discount.

Truth: Often not. The Good Driver Discount is mandated, but good-student, mileage, bundling, and course credits frequently require you to ask and provide proof.

Myth: One ticket or claim ruins my rate forever.

Truth: Surcharges fade over time, and a clean ~3-year record restores the Good Driver Discount. Shopping around after an incident usually finds a better-priced insurer.

8 Coverages & key terms

The coverages on a California policy, in plain terms. The note on each is the California-specific point to remember. Bay Area drivers should pay particular attention to comprehensive (theft is elevated in many cities) and UM/UIM.

Bodily Injury & Property Damage liability
Pays for injuries and property you cause others. The required 30/60/15 limits are the minimum. California: Required statewide. Most drivers should buy more than the minimum.
Collision
Repairs or replaces your own car after a crash, regardless of fault. California: Optional, but required by lenders. Weigh it against your car’s value.
Comprehensive
Covers your car for theft, vandalism, fire, falling objects, and weather. California: Worth more in Bay Area cities (theft) and wildfire/storm-exposed areas.
Uninsured / Underinsured Motorist (UM/UIM)
Pays your bills when an at-fault driver has no insurance or too little — including hit-and-runs. California: Insurers must offer it; you can reject it only in writing. With ~1 in 6 drivers uninsured, keep it.
Medical Payments (MedPay)
Covers medical bills for you and your passengers regardless of fault. California: Optional. California has no no-fault/PIP, so MedPay is the main first-party medical add-on.
GAP
If a financed car is totaled, pays the difference between the loan balance and the car’s value. California: Useful on new, financed, or leased cars that depreciate quickly.
Deductible
What you pay out of pocket on a collision or comprehensive claim before coverage applies. California: A higher deductible lowers your premium — pick an amount you could pay today.
Good Driver Discount
A California-mandated discount of at least 20% for qualifying drivers. California: Requires ~3 years’ experience and a clean record (no more than one point).

Coverage is a balance between premium and risk. Here is how the main trade-offs play out in California.

Illustrative. Raising your deductible lowers the premium but increases what you pay after a claim.

Rules of thumb

  • Buy more than 30/60/15. State minimums are low. If you have any assets, 100/300/100 bodily-injury limits are a common, affordable step up.
  • Keep uninsured-motorist coverage. California lets you waive it in writing, but with so many uninsured drivers it’s one of the best values on the policy.
  • Use the 10% rule on older cars. If collision + comprehensive plus the deductible exceed ~10% of the car’s value, consider dropping them.
  • Lean on California’s mileage factor. Because miles driven is a mandatory factor, low-mileage and verified-mileage programs can meaningfully cut your rate.
  • Earn and keep the Good Driver Discount. Three clean years and you qualify for at least 20% off — protect it.
  • Re-shop after any rate hike. Approved rate changes vary by insurer. The same coverage can differ by hundreds of dollars a year.

11 What to do after an accident

If you’re in a crash in California, what you do in the first minutes and days matters — for safety, for the law, and for your claim. Here’s exactly what to do.

Step by step

  1. Stop and stay safe. Never leave the scene — leaving an injury crash is a felony in California. If the cars are drivable and it’s safe, move them out of traffic and turn on hazards. Check everyone for injuries.
  2. Call 911 if anyone’s hurt or roads are blocked. Get medical help and police on scene for any injury, death, or major damage. A police report is invaluable for your claim even when officers don’t always respond to minor fender-benders.
  3. Exchange information. Get the other driver’s name, phone, address, driver’s license number, license plate, and insurance company + policy number. Note the car’s make/model/color.
  4. Document everything. Photograph all vehicles, damage, positions, skid marks, street signs, and the overall scene. Get names and numbers of any witnesses. Write down the time, location, and what happened while it’s fresh.
  5. Don’t admit fault or over-share. Be polite but don’t say ‘it was my fault’ or speculate — fault is for the insurers and, in California’s at-fault system, can be shared. Stick to facts when speaking with the other driver or police.
  6. Report it to your insurer promptly. Call your own insurer (or use its app) as soon as you safely can, even if you weren’t at fault. Prompt notice is usually required by your policy. Give facts; let them handle liability.
  7. Get medical attention — and keep records. See a doctor even if you feel fine; some injuries surface later. Keep all bills and records. In California, MedPay (if you bought it) helps regardless of fault, since the state has no PIP.
  8. Know the California reporting rules. You must file form SR-1 with the DMV within 10 days if anyone is injured/killed or property damage exceeds $1,000 (nearly every crash). This is separate from any police or insurance report.

Don't

  • Don’t leave the scene — it can turn a fender-bender into a crime.
  • Don’t admit fault or apologize in a way that assigns blame.
  • Don’t accept cash to ‘skip insurance’ — hidden injuries and damage can cost you later.
  • Don’t sign anything from the other driver or a non-police party at the scene.
  • Don’t forget the SR-1 — the DMV deadline is 10 days and missing it can suspend your license.

Keep in your glovebox

  • Insurance card (in the glovebox + a photo on your phone)
  • Driver’s license & registration
  • Phone for photos and calls
  • Pen & paper or a notes app
  • Emergency contacts
  • Roadside/tow number

12 Help & resources

Official California programs and Bay Area resources. These are the places to verify requirements, find low-cost options, and get local assistance.

  • California Department of Insurance — Regulator under Prop 103. Compare rates, check a company or agent’s license, and file a complaint. Consumer hotline 1-800-927-4357. insurance.ca.gov
  • California Low Cost Auto (CLCA) — State program providing affordable liability insurance to income-eligible good drivers with a vehicle valued at $25,000 or less. mylowcostauto.com
  • Good Driver Discount — Prop 103 guarantees at least 20% off for drivers with ~3 years’ experience and a clean record. Ask every insurer if you qualify. Prop 103 guide
  • California DMV — insurance requirements — Proof-of-insurance rules, electronic reporting, and what happens to your registration if coverage lapses. dmv.ca.gov
  • Consumer Watchdog — The nonprofit behind Prop 103. Tracks rate filings and challenges increases on behalf of California drivers. consumerwatchdog.org
  • Bay Area DMV field offices — Find your nearest Bay Area DMV office for registration, license, and SR-1 filing. Locations in SF, Oakland, San Jose, and across all nine counties. dmv.ca.gov/offices
